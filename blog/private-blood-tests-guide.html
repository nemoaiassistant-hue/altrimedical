Why Private Blood Tests Are Worth Considering

By Dr Abad · 28 May 2026 · 4 min read
A

Dr Abad

GMC-registered doctor and Lead Aesthetic Doctor at Altri Medical Ltd, Altrincham

In my daily practice, I see a growing number of patients who want to take a more proactive approach to their health. They do not want to wait until symptoms appear before understanding what is happening inside their body. This shift towards preventive health is one of the most positive trends I have observed, and private blood testing is central to it.

The Case for Proactive Health Screening

The traditional healthcare model in the UK is largely reactive — you visit your GP when something is wrong, and tests are ordered to investigate symptoms. This approach works well for acute problems, but it is not ideal for identifying subtle, developing issues before they become clinically apparent.

Many common health conditions develop silently over years. Elevated cholesterol, pre-diabetic blood sugar changes, underactive thyroid, vitamin deficiencies, and early markers of inflammation can all be present long before you notice any symptoms. By the time symptoms do appear, the condition may be more advanced and harder to reverse.

Prevention is not about anxiety or over-testing — it is about information. A comprehensive blood test gives you a clear picture of your internal health, empowering you and your doctor to make informed decisions before problems develop.

Key Blood Panels to Consider

At our clinic, I recommend targeted panels based on each patient's age, sex, medical history, and concerns. The following are the panels I most frequently request:

Full Blood Count (FBC)

The cornerstone of any blood assessment. A full blood count measures red blood cells, white blood cells, platelets, haemoglobin, and haematocrit. It can reveal anaemia, infection, immune system issues, and certain blood disorders.

  • Low haemoglobin → possible iron deficiency or anaemia
  • Abnormal white cell count → infection, inflammation, or immune concerns
  • Low platelets → potential clotting or bone marrow issues

Thyroid Function (TSH, Free T4, Free T3)

Thyroid disorders are remarkably common — particularly among women — and frequently underdiagnosed. Symptoms like fatigue, weight gain, hair loss, low mood, and brain fog can all be caused by an underactive thyroid (hypothyroidism). A full thyroid panel checks TSH, free T4, and ideally free T3, giving a comprehensive picture of thyroid function.

  • Elevated TSH → possible underactive thyroid
  • Low free T4 → reduced thyroid hormone production
  • Suboptimal free T3 → poor conversion of T4 to active T3

Hormone Panel

For both men and women, a hormone assessment can reveal imbalances that affect energy, mood, body composition, and overall wellbeing.

  • Women: Oestradiol, progesterone, FSH, LH (assessing menstrual health, perimenopause, and menopause)
  • Men: Total and free testosterone, SHBG, prolactin (assessing energy, libido, muscle mass, and mood)
  • Both: DHEA-S and cortisol for adrenal function

Vitamin and Mineral Status

Deficiencies are far more common than most people realise, particularly in the UK where sunlight exposure is limited for much of the year.

  • Vitamin D: estimated to affect 1 in 5 adults in the UK; linked to bone health, immune function, mood, and energy
  • Vitamin B12: deficiency common in vegetarians, vegans, and those with digestive conditions
  • Folate (B9): essential for cell division and particularly important for women planning pregnancy
  • Ferritin (iron stores): low ferritin can cause fatigue, hair loss, and poor concentration even with normal haemoglobin

Metabolic and Cardiovascular Markers

  • Lipid profile: total cholesterol, LDL, HDL, and triglycerides — cardiovascular risk assessment
  • HbA1c: average blood sugar over the preceding 3 months; screens for pre-diabetes and diabetes
  • Fasting glucose and insulin: for a more detailed metabolic assessment
  • hs-CRP: high-sensitivity C-reactive protein, a marker of systemic inflammation
  • Uric acid: linked to gout and cardiovascular risk

How Often Should You Test?

The frequency depends on your age, health status, and personal goals. As a general guide:

  • Ages 20–30: A baseline comprehensive panel, then every 2–3 years unless symptoms arise
  • Ages 30–40: Every 1–2 years, with thyroid and hormone panels if relevant
  • Ages 40–50: Annually, with particular attention to metabolic markers and cardiovascular risk
  • Ages 50+: Annually, with expanded panels including prostate-specific antigen (PSA) for men

More frequent testing may be warranted if you are starting a new supplement regime, beginning a weight loss programme, undergoing hormonal treatment, or monitoring a known condition.

NHS vs Private: What Is the Difference?

The NHS provides excellent care, but its resources are finite. NHS blood tests are typically ordered to investigate specific symptoms and are subject to clinical thresholds. If your results fall within the "normal" reference range — even if that range is very broad — further investigation may not be offered.

Private blood testing offers several advantages:

  • Broader panels: You can request comprehensive screening rather than symptom-specific tests
  • Faster results: Private results are typically available within 24–72 hours, compared to potential weeks on the NHS
  • Detailed interpretation: A private consultation allows time for thorough discussion of results, including "suboptimal" values that may not trigger NHS follow-up but are worth addressing preventively
  • No wait times: Appointments are available at your convenience without referral delays
  • Continuity: Results are tracked over time, building a personal health profile that makes it easier to spot trends
I see private blood testing as complementary to NHS care, not a replacement. The NHS remains your primary resource for acute illness and chronic disease management. Private testing fills the gap for proactive, preventive health monitoring.

What Results Can Reveal Early

Some of the most impactful early findings from routine blood testing include:

  • Pre-diabetes: Elevated HbA1c can be detected years before type 2 diabetes develops, giving you time to make lifestyle changes
  • Subclinical hypothyroidism: Borderline thyroid results may explain fatigue, weight gain, and low mood that have been dismissed
  • Liver strain: Elevated liver enzymes (ALT, AST, GGT) can indicate early liver stress from diet, alcohol, or medication
  • Kidney function decline: Subtle changes in eGFR and creatinine can be detected and monitored
  • Inflammatory markers: Elevated hs-CRP may suggest chronic inflammation that warrants further investigation

The Bottom Line

Knowledge is the foundation of good health. A comprehensive blood test is one of the most cost-effective investments you can make in your long-term wellbeing. It is not about looking for problems — it is about understanding your baseline, identifying areas for optimisation, and having the information to make proactive decisions.
