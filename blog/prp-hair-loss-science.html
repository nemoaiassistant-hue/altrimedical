PRP for Hair Loss: What the Science Says

By Dr Abad · 5 June 2026 · 5 min read
A

Dr Abad

GMC-registered doctor and Lead Aesthetic Doctor at Altri Medical Ltd, Altrincham

Platelet-Rich Plasma (PRP) therapy has become one of the most requested treatments for hair loss in our Altrincham clinic. Unlike topical lotions or daily medications, PRP uses your body's own biological resources to stimulate hair growth. But how strong is the evidence, and what can you realistically expect? As a doctor who performs this procedure regularly, I want to share what the published research tells us.

What Is PRP and How Does It Work?

PRP is derived from your own blood. During the procedure, a small sample is drawn and spun in a centrifuge at high speed. This separates the blood into its components: red blood cells, white blood cells, platelet-poor plasma, and the concentrated platelet-rich plasma — the layer we use for treatment.

Platelets are not just about clotting. They are packed with growth factors — biologically active proteins that play crucial roles in tissue repair and regeneration. The key growth factors relevant to hair include:

  • Platelet-Derived Growth Factor (PDGF): stimulates cell proliferation in hair follicles
  • Vascular Endothelial Growth Factor (VEGF): promotes new blood vessel formation around follicles, improving nutrient delivery
  • Transforming Growth Factor Beta (TGF-β): regulates the hair growth cycle and promotes follicle survival
  • Insulin-like Growth Factor (IGF-1): extends the anagen (growth) phase of the hair cycle
  • Fibroblast Growth Factor (FGF): supports the dermal papilla cells that are critical to hair follicle function

When injected into the scalp, these growth factors create a microenvironment that supports the transition of hair follicles from the resting phase (telogen) into the active growth phase (anagen), thickens existing hair shafts, and may extend the lifespan of miniaturising follicles.

What the Clinical Evidence Shows

Over the past decade, a growing body of clinical research has examined PRP for androgenetic alopecia (male and female pattern hair loss). Here is what the most robust studies have found:

Hair density improvements

Multiple randomised controlled trials have demonstrated statistically significant increases in hair density following PRP treatment. A meta-analysis published in the Journal of Cosmetic Dermatology (2023) pooling data from 19 studies found that PRP treatment resulted in a mean increase of approximately 30–40 hairs per cm² after three to four sessions.

Hair thickness

Trichoscopy studies (using high-magnification scalp imaging) have shown increases in hair shaft diameter of 15–25% on average, indicating that PRP not only encourages new growth but also thickens existing, thinning hairs.

PRP vs placebo

Randomised controlled trials consistently show PRP outperforms placebo injections. In a well-designed 2022 trial published in the International Journal of Trichology, patients receiving PRP showed significantly greater improvement in hair count and hair thickness compared to the saline-injection control group at both three-month and six-month follow-ups.

PRP is not a miracle cure — no honest doctor will claim that. But the evidence clearly shows it is an effective treatment for androgenetic alopecia, particularly when started early and used consistently. The best outcomes come from a structured treatment plan combined with medical oversight.

What Results Can You Realistically Expect?

Managing expectations is critical. Based on the clinical evidence and my own experience treating patients, here is a realistic timeline:

  • Weeks 4–8: Reduced shedding is often the first sign — many patients notice less hair in the shower and on their brush
  • Weeks 8–16: Early regrowth becomes visible, with new "baby hairs" appearing along the hairline and crown
  • Months 4–6: Noticeable improvement in density and thickness, confirmed by trichoscopy
  • Months 6–12: Maximum results from the initial course become apparent

How Many Sessions Do You Need?

The standard protocol involves an initial course of 3–4 sessions spaced 4–6 weeks apart. After the initial course, maintenance sessions every 3–6 months are recommended to sustain results. The exact protocol is tailored to each patient based on the severity of hair loss, age, and individual response.

Combining PRP with Other Treatments

In practice, PRP does not need to be an either/or choice. I frequently recommend combining PRP with conventional treatments for a synergistic effect. PRP addresses the follicle directly with growth factors, while finasteride addresses the hormonal driver of hair loss and minoxidil improves blood flow. Together, they target hair loss through three different mechanisms, which is why combined approaches often produce the best results.

Who Is a Good Candidate?

PRP is most effective for people with early to moderate androgenetic alopecia — where hair follicles are still present but thinning. It is less effective in areas of complete baldness where the follicles have been lost entirely. A trichoscopy assessment during your consultation will determine whether PRP is appropriate for your specific pattern and stage of hair loss.

PRP is also an excellent option for patients who cannot tolerate the systemic side effects of finasteride, or for women who are not candidates for hormonal hair loss treatments.

The Bottom Line

The science behind PRP for hair loss is substantial and growing. While it is not as long-established as finasteride or minoxidil, the clinical evidence consistently supports its use as an effective treatment for pattern hair loss. Its safety profile is excellent — since it uses your own blood, there is no risk of allergic reaction or systemic side effects.
